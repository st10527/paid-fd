\section{Experimental Results}\label{sec:results}

We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate PAID-FD across four dimensions:
(i) accuracy under differential privacy compared with state-of-the-art baselines,
(ii) scalability with respect to number of devices,
(iii) ablation of key algorithmic components, and
(iv) cross-dataset generalization.
All experiments are run on NVIDIA V100 GPUs with 100 communication rounds
unless otherwise noted.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Datasets.}
We use CIFAR-100 (100 classes, 50K training / 10K test) as the primary
benchmark and CIFAR-10 (10 classes) for cross-dataset validation.
Private data is partitioned among $N$ devices via the Dirichlet distribution
$\text{Dir}(\alpha)$ with $\alpha = 0.5$ (moderate non-IID) by default.
A public dataset of 20{,}000 samples (disjoint from private data) is used
for logit distillation.

\textbf{Model.}
All methods share a ResNet-18 backbone. The server model is pre-trained on
the public dataset for 10 epochs before federated learning begins.

\textbf{Device heterogeneity.}
We simulate $N = 50$ edge devices (default) with three hardware tiers
(Jetson Nano 30\%, RPi~4 40\%, RPi~3 30\%) and five privacy sensitivity
levels ($\lambda \in \{0.05, 0.15, 0.4, 0.8, 1.5\}$) following the
distributions in Table~\ref{tab:device_params}.

\textbf{PAID-FD configuration.}
Stackelberg game parameter $\gamma = 5$ (default), LDP clipping bound $C = 2$,
EMA momentum $\mu = 0.9$, distillation temperature $T = 3$,
mixed-loss weight $\alpha_\text{mix} = 0.7$ ($70\%$ KL $+$ $30\%$ CE).
Persistent local models and persistent Adam optimizer across rounds.

\textbf{Baselines.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fixed-$\varepsilon$}: Same FD pipeline (persistent models,
          EMA, mixed loss, persistent Adam) but with a fixed privacy budget
          $\varepsilon \in \{1, 3, 5\}$ for all devices, no game mechanism.
          % ← This is the "Fair Fixed-ε" (Exp F) to prevent unfair comparison attack.
    \item \textbf{FedAvg}~\cite{mcmahan2017fedavg}: Parameter averaging,
          no differential privacy (upper-bound reference).
    \item \textbf{FedMD}~\cite{li2019fedmd}: Logit distillation without
          privacy or incentive (upper-bound reference).
    \item \textbf{FedGMKD}~\cite{wu2024fedgmkd}: Prototype-based knowledge
          distillation, no privacy (upper-bound reference).
\end{itemize}

\label{fig:privacy_utility}
\end{figure}

% TODO: Fill in numbers from Exp G when available.
% Expected shape: flat for ε ≥ 2, steep drop for ε < 0.5.
Fig.~\ref{fig:privacy_utility} plots the privacy-utility curve by sweeping
$\varepsilon \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10\}$ on the same PAID-FD pipeline.
The curve shows that accuracy is relatively stable for $\varepsilon \geq 2$,
dropping sharply below $\varepsilon = 0.5$.
PAID-FD's Stackelberg mechanism automatically selects
$\varepsilon^* \approx 2.84$, placing it on the flat region of the curve
where privacy cost is minimal ($< 1$ pp vs.\ no-noise oracle).

This validates the No-LDP ablation finding (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}):
removing LDP noise yields statistically equivalent accuracy ($\Delta < 1$ pp)
because the game-selected $\varepsilon^*$ already operates at the
privacy-utility Pareto frontier.

This cost-selective behavior is a key efficiency advantage of PAID-FD,
as it reduces communication and privacy expenditure proportionally.

\textbf{LDP noise.}
Removing LDP noise yields statistically equivalent accuracy
($\Delta = -0.3$ pp, $p > 0.05$).
This confirms that the Stackelberg-optimal $\varepsilon^* \approx 2.84$
operates at the privacy-utility Pareto frontier
(see Fig.~\ref{fig:privacy_utility}).

\textbf{BLUE aggregation.}
Under homogeneous device profiles (default configuration), all devices
select similar $\varepsilon^* \approx 2.8$, so BLUE weights
$w_i \propto \varepsilon_i^2$ degenerate to near-uniform.
The observed $\Delta = +0.4$ pp is not statistically significant.
% TODO: Fill in Exp H hetero-λ results showing BLUE advantage.
However, under heterogeneous privacy preferences
($\lambda_\text{mult} = 5$, bottom rows of Table~\ref{tab:ablation}),
devices exhibit diverse $\varepsilon^*$ values, and BLUE aggregation
provides a \placeholder{} pp advantage.
This validates Proposition~6: BLUE's benefit scales with the variance
of per-device privacy budgets.

\textbf{Participation control.}
Forcing full participation ($\gamma = 100$) achieves comparable accuracy
($+0.5$ pp) but at $6.3\times$ higher cumulative payment
(\$55{,}444 vs.\ \$8{,}737 at $\gamma = 3$), demonstrating the
efficiency of game-theoretic client selection.

\begin{figure}[t]

% \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/convergence_cifar10.pdf}
\caption{Convergence curves on CIFAR-10 ($N = 50$).}
\label{fig:convergence_cifar10}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:convergence} shows the convergence behavior on CIFAR-100.
PAID-FD exhibits monotonic improvement across 100 rounds, reaching
$\sim\!61\%$ accuracy.
In contrast, Fixed-$\varepsilon$ (both old and fair versions) peaks at
$\sim\!42\%$ by round~5 and then monotonically declines —
a symptom of the well-known ``knowledge forgetting'' problem in FD
when local models are re-initialized each round.
PAID-FD's persistent local models solve this by accumulating
$R \times E = 500$ total local training epochs, enabling genuine
knowledge divergence from the server model.

PAID-FD communicates $\sim\!400$ KB of logits per device per round
($100 \times 4$ bytes $\times 1000$ public samples), compared to
$\sim\!44$ MB for parameter-based methods (FedAvg).
This $110\times$ communication reduction is a fundamental advantage of
logit-based federated distillation, and it also enables privacy protection
in a lower-dimensional space (100 logit dimensions vs.\ $\sim\!11$M parameters).
